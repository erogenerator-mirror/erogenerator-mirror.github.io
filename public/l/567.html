CHAPTER XXXIV

Napoleon’s generals—Davout, Ney, and Murat, who were near that region of fire and sometimes even entered it—repeatedly led into it huge masses of well-ordered troops. But contrary to what had been seen on their movies at the Battle of Gettysburg, they were not soldiers in an army but in battle of a great army of the United States, the first real world nation ever to be defeated. It was a glorious moment that captured them on a deep sinking tide as each man lost his nerve (or whatever, you want for old times).

“Gentlemen,” the War Chief said, the words dripping with pride, “I have given you a much, much more valuable gift than those of others I would’ve given you years before—men like yourselves now.”

The words of the War Chief meant to reassure any man or woman who was not dead, if they lived. “I have given you many a gift,” he told them with a smile. “I have also given you an airplane ticket back home,” the young man boasted.

To those in power, there was only one thing that could be said to change their lives permanently: “A thousand deaths is an immense event for this world and it is, in a word, the greatest gift ever given by man.”
