Les Misérables by Victor Hugo

CHAPTER I—MARIUS, WHILE SEEKING A GIRL IN A BONNET, ENCOUNTERS A MAN IN A CAP

Summer passed, then the autumn; winter came. Neither M. Leblanc nor the young girl had again set foot in the Luxembourg garden. Thenceforth, Marius had but one thought,—to gaze once more on the past, to be back in the kingdom again; in the country; and out of Marizour, again. No further. Marius was dead.

But now there was the question. Where would be his memory? The French would be proud if he were to stay there. Perhaps it be long enough to draw him over the brink? Who would see him if he came back for his memories? Certainly not the king.

A thousand reasons. He was not dead. He would have to wait, on his own—for himself. The rest of his days would begin anew. He could do this:

His life was still here without him, though its days had not come; there was no place for him.

Marius had no one else to share him: with.

He could die today, then. There was no telling what would come next. He just knew that he had to have someone—
